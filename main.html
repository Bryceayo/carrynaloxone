How Naloxone Works

It's simpler than you think. One spray could save someone's life.

01
üß†

Opioids Block Breathing

Opioids bind to receptors in your brain that control breathing. During an overdose, breathing slows or stops completely‚Äîthat's why skin turns blue.

02
üíä

Naloxone Kicks Them Off

Naloxone binds to those same receptors and knocks the opioids off. It's like a reset button that lets the brain start controlling breathing again.

03
üí®

Breathing Restarts

Within 2-3 minutes, the person can start breathing again. This buys crucial time for emergency services to arrive.

Safe If You're Wrong

Used naloxone on someone who wasn't overdosing? Nothing happens. Zero side effects. It only affects opioids in the system.

Worried about getting in trouble if you call 911? Don't be. Oklahoma law protects you.

  • You cannot be prosecuted for drug possession at the scene if you call 911 to help someone overdosing
  • The person overdosing is also protected from possession charges
  • This applies even if you've taken something yourself‚Äîthe law wants you to make the call
  • Protection applies when you stay and cooperate with emergency responders

Reference: Oklahoma Good Samaritan Law, 63 O.S. ¬ß 2-413.1
