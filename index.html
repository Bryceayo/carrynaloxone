How Naloxone Works

It's simpler than you think. One spray could save someone's life.

01
üß†

Opioids Block Breathing

Opioids bind to receptors in your brain that control breathing. During an overdose, breathing slows or stops completely, which is why the skin turns blue.

02
üíä

Naloxone Kicks Them Off

Naloxone binds to those same receptors and knocks the opioids off. It's like a reset button that lets the brain start controlling breathing again.

03
üí®

Breathing Restarts

Within 2-3 minutes, the person can start breathing again. This buys crucial time for emergency services to arrive.

Safe If You're Wrong

Used naloxone on someone who wasn't overdosing? Nothing happens. Zero side effects. It only affects opioids in the system.

üõ°Ô∏è Oklahoma's Good Samaritan Law Protects You

Worried about getting in trouble if you call 911? Don't be. Oklahoma law protects you.

  • You cannot be prosecuted for drug possession at the scene if you call 911 to help someone overdosing
  • The person overdosing is also protected from possession charges
  • This applies even if you've taken something yourself. The law wants you to make the call
  • Protection applies when you stay and cooperate with emergency responders

Reference: Oklahoma Good Samaritan Law, 63 O.S. ¬ß 2-413.1

Common Questions

Everything you need to know about carrying and using naloxone.

Nothing happens. Naloxone only affects opioids in someone's system. If there are no opioids present, there are no side effects whatsoever. It's like using an EpiPen on someone who isn't having an allergic reaction. No harm done.
No. 90% of fentanyl overdoses in Oklahoma come from pills that people thought were something else: Xanax, Percocet, or other pressed pills. You're not expecting drug use. You're prepared for an emergency that can happen to anyone. It's like carrying a first aid kit.
Look for: blue or grayish lips and fingertips, slow or stopped breathing, unresponsiveness (won't wake up to loud sounds or pain), small "pinpoint" pupils, and gurgling or choking sounds. If you're unsure, administer naloxone anyway. It won't hurt them.
Naloxone works for 30-90 minutes. Some opioids (especially fentanyl) can last longer, so the person might start overdosing again. That's why calling 911 is critical. They need medical monitoring even after naloxone works.
Research consistently shows that naloxone access does not increase drug use. We don't say EpiPens enable people to eat foods they're allergic to, or that seatbelts enable reckless driving. Naloxone is a safety tool. Nothing more.
Somewhere accessible: your backpack, car glovebox, purse, nightstand, or medicine cabinet. Keep it at room temperature and out of direct sunlight. Check the expiration date occasionally. Expired naloxone may still work, but it's less effective.
